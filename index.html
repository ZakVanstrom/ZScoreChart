Z-Scores and P-Values:

Definition:

Z-Scores ("Standard Scores") are a unit of Standard Deviation. They help with calculations of probability and distribution for Normal populations.

Formulas:

If you have:
        Standard Deviation  -> σ    (Sigma, symbol for Standard Deviation) 
        Mean (average)           -> μ    (Mu, symbol for Mean)
        Any Value                       -> x    (As long as population doesn't exclude it)
Then you have a Z-Score, where:
        z = ( x - μ ) / σ 

If you have:
        Sample Standard Deviation  -> s
        Sample Mean                                -> x̄    (x-bar)
        Any Value                                        -> x
Then you also have a Z-Score, where:
        z = (x - x̄ ) / s        (They're the same thing)

Purpose:

What do they do?    Z-Scores are helpful to: 
    -    Show distance from the mean
    -    Compare a result with it's population

In English?
Z-Scores tell you how far a value is from average

Example:

Like how? Let's try this.
Your math class had an exam. 
Let's say you got 98 of 100 points on the test.
Your teacher announces that the class average was 78 of 100 points. 
From the class's scores, she shared that the Standard Deviation is 10 points. 

Using your newfound knowledge, you can see that: 
    - σ (class's standard deviation = 10 points
    - μ (class average) = 78 points
    - x (your score) = 98 points
            Then, you can use your formula:

    z = ( x - μ ) / σ    -->    z = (98 - 78) / 10
            >> z = (20) / 10
            >> z = 2

So, your Z-Score is 2, but what does that mean?
    In short, z = 2 means that:
        --> Your score was better than around 98% of students
        --> Only 2% of students performed better than you

These percentages are calculated using the "Empirical Rule" (also called the "68-95-99.7% Rule")  which really isn't as hard as it sounds
